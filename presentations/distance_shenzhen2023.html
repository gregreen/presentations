Eratosthenes Measures the Earth

(image adapted from: Wikimedia / Mrmw)

Calculate Earth's circumference: $$ C = \frac{2\pi}{\gamma} \Delta G $$

Eratosthenes' measurements:

  • $\Delta G = 5000\,\mathrm{stadia}$
  • $\gamma = 2\pi / 50$

⇒ Circumference = 250,000 stadia

How accurate was Eratosthenes' measurement?

Maybe better than 1%, but we don't know how long a stadion was!

  2. Measure the angles ($\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$).

  • Parallax measurement. Projection of Venus onto the Sun from two points on Earth.
  • Link an angle to a distance we can measure on Earth.

Earth-Sun Distance: The Transit of Venus

(courtesy of NASA / Solar Dynamics Observatory)

  • Imagine looking at Venus in front of Sun, and moving your head up and down.
  • In reality, the difference in angles is very small, so difficult to detect.
  • But the time of transit changes as you move the path up or down along the Sun.
  • The difference is a few minutes, which is measurable.

Earth-Sun Distance: The Transit of Venus

(map from Wikimedia / AlexiusHoratius)

  • Edmund Halley proposed observing transit from many places on Earth.
  • One pair of transits approximately every century.
  • 1761 and 1769.
  • People traveled all over the world to observe the transit. Captain Cook was sent to Tahiti in 1769.
  • The astronomical unit was measured to be 153 million km - better than 1% accuracy!

Distances to Stars: Stellar Parallax (恒星视差)

(image credit: Wikimedia / Srain)

1 parsec (pc, 秒差距) ≃ 206264 AU

≃ 3.086×1013 km

≃ 3.26 light years

Astronomers like to use parsecs.

  • As we orbit the Sun, our vantage point moves.
  • Stars appear to go around loops on sky.
  • The closer the star, the larger the loop.
  • Parallax gives us a distance, tied to the Earth-Sun distance. This is tied to observations of the transit of Venus, which is tied to distances measured on the Earth, and ultimately to the length of some king's foot! A complete chain of measurements.

(video credit: ESA/Gaia/DPAC)

(image credit: BMF / Hermann Schwahn / Johann Eduard Wolff)

First published parallax measurement (1838)

61 Cygni: 0.314 arcsec

$$ \mathrm{distance} = \frac{1\,\mathrm{parsec}}{\mathrm{parallax} / \mathrm{arcsec}} = 3.2\,\mathrm{parsec} $$

(correct value: 0.286 arcsec, 3.5 parsec)

  • Three different astronomers all measured parallaxes around the same time: Friedrich Bessel, Wilhelm Struve and Thomas Henderson.
  • Bessel published first. This happens often in science.
  • Required many very careful measurements of apparent separations between stars over the course of years.
  • Bessel's measurement was accurate to ~10%. Pretty good!

What if parallax is too small to measure?

(image credit: Wikimedia / Richard Powell)

Using our knowledge of the distances to stars, we can learn about their properties.

Herzsprung-Russell Diagram: stellar temperature (温度) vs. luminosity (光度)

Types of stars: main-sequence stars, giants, white dwarfs, ...

  • What about when stars are so far away that we can't measure parallax?
  • Can find patterns in properties of nearby stars with parallax distances.
  • Use these properties to determine distances of more distant stars.
  • Surface temperature vs. Luminosity: Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram.

Cepheid Variable Stars (变亮星): 𝜷 Doradus

(data: Plachy et al 2021)

Period-Luminosity Relation (周期亮度比例)

(figure: Shapley 1949)

Brighter stars have longer periods.

⇒ Can measure distance from period and apparent brightness of Cepheid.

Discovered by Henrietta Leavitt in 1912:

“Standard Candle” (标准烛光)

$$ \mathrm{apparent\ brightness} = \frac{\mathrm{luminosity}}{\mathrm{distance}^2} $$

$$ \implies \mathrm{distance} = \sqrt{\frac{\mathrm{luminosity}}{\mathrm{apparent\ brightness}}} $$

(image based on Wikimedia / BGN-WMCO)
