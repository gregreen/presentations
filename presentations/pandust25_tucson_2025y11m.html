Modeling dust far-infrared emission

Modified blackbody model:

$$ I_{\nu} \left( \nu , T , \beta \right) = \overbrace{\sigma}^{\mathrm{column}\\ \mathrm{density}} \!\!\!\! \underbrace{ \kappa \left( \nu , \beta \right) }_{ \mathrm{low-}\nu\ \mathrm{cutoff} \\ \ \ \ \ (\kappa \ \propto \ \nu^{\beta}) } \! \overbrace{B_{\nu} \left( \nu , T \right)}^{\mathrm{blackbody}} \, . \ $$

Assume extinction proportional to $\sigma$.

Calibrate using sources with known extinction.

Shortcomings of emission-based maps

  • 2D — How far away is the dust?
  • Based on emission, but used to correct for extinction.
  • Assumptions about dust temperature and emissivity.
  • Contamination (cosmic infrared background, zodiacal light, galaxies, ...)

Want a better map: 3D and based on extinction, which is what we use dust maps to correct for.

Average stellar color vs. distance traces dust extinction. Complication: Stars have different intrinsic colors!

The “right way” to make a 3D dust map

1. Create forward model of dust & stars.

Dust density in 3D: $\rho_{\mathrm{dust}}\left(x,y,z\right)$

Extinction: $A\left(\ell,b,r\right) = \int_0^r \rho_{\mathrm{dust}}\left(\ell,b,r^{\prime}\right) \, \mathrm{d}r^{\prime}$

Stellar flux: $f_{\nu}\left(\lambda\right) = F_{\nu}\left(\lambda,\theta\right) r^{-2} 10^{-0.4 A}$

2. Use observed stellar colors (+parallaxes, spectra, etc.) to constrain the model.

Write down likelihoods & priors.

Run MCMC (or your favorite inference algorithm).

Profit.

What about the extinction curve?

Almost all dust maps to date essentially measure dust density: $$ \rho_{\mathrm{dust}}\left(\ell,b,r\right) \sim \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r} \underbrace{ \vphantom{\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}} A\left(\ell, b, r\right) }_{ \substack{\mathrm{extinction\ in}\\\mathrm{some\ band}\vphantom{\large X}} } \, . $$

The extinction curve $A(\lambda, \ell, b, r)$ contains information about the physical properties of dust.

Key takeaways

1. 3D maps of dust extinction density have come a long way, and are now very good.

2. The new frontier: Mapping the dust extinction curve with large surveys of low-resolution spectra to learn about dust properties.

3. By correlating dust extinction curve with other ISM properties, we can learn about the nature of dust and its role in the ISM.
