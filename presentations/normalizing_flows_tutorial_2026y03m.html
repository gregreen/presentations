Motivation: Why model distributions?

Distributions in astrophysics:

  • Physical distributions: initial mass function, stellar metallicity distribution, ...
  • Observational uncertainties: Gaussian errors, Poisson noise, ...
  • Statistical inference: Likelihoods, posteriors, priors, ...

What AI researchers are interested in: much higher-dimensional objects.

  • Images: Every pixel is a dimension.
  • Language: Sequences of words or tokens.

“The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy ...”

⇒ p(next word = X) for all possible words X.

What we want to do with distributions

  • Sample from the distribution.
  • Evaluate the probability density at a given point.
  • Ask whether a given dataset follows a given distribution (“Is the CMB Gaussian?”).
  • More advanced: Compute gradients of the probability with respect to the parameters.

Modeling distributions: Variational inference

Posterior distribution: $p(\theta \mid D)$, where $\theta$ are model parameters and $D$ is the observed data.

Instead of trying to directly compute the posterior everywhere, approximate it using a class of simple distributions: $$ p(\theta \mid D) \approx q(\theta) \, . $$

For example, $q(\theta)$ could be a Gaussian with mean and covariance to be determined.

Minimize some measure of the difference between $p(\theta \mid D)$ and $q(\theta)$.

The class of distributions used for $q(\theta)$ should be flexible enough to capture complex posterior distributions, but it should be easy to work with (i.e., evaluation, sampling, marginalization).

Sampling from a 1D distribution

In 1D, we can sample from a distribution by inverting the cumulative probability function:

First, model the cumulative distribution function (CDF): $$ P(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x p(x') dx' \, . $$

Then, sample a uniform random variable $u \sim \mathrm{U}(0, 1)$, and apply the inverse CDF: $$ F^{-1}(u) \sim p(x) \, . $$

However, this doesn't work in higher dimensions: There is no such thing as a multivariate CDF (because vectors can't be ordered).

Sampling from a >1D distribution

Kernel Density Estimation (KDE): Smooth over the points with a kernel function (e.g., a Gaussian).

Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): Model the distribution as a sum of Gaussians.

Curse of dimensionality: The number of samples needed to accurately model the distribution grows exponentially with the number of dimensions.

Tuning: KDEs require tuning a bandwidth parameter. GMMs require tuning the number of components.

Normalizing flows

Normalizing flows: Theory

Idea: Start with a simple distribution (e.g., a Gaussian), and apply an invertible coordinate transformation $F$.

Sampling: Sample from the simple distribution, and apply the transformations to the sample: $$ \vec{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\vec{0}, \mathbb{1}) \, . \\ \vec{y} = F(\vec{x}) \sim p_y(\vec{y}) \, . $$

Density evaluation: Apply the inverse transformation and use the change-of-variables formula: $$ \begin{align} p_y(\vec{y}) &= p_x(\vec{x}) \left| \det \left( \frac{\partial \vec{x}}{\partial \vec{y}} \right) \right| = \mathcal{N}(F^{-1}(\vec{y}) \mid \vec{0}, \mathbb{1}) \left| \det \left( \frac{\partial F^{-1}(\vec{y})}{\partial \vec{y}} \right) \right| \, . \end{align} $$

The transformation $F$ should be as flexible as possible, while remaining invertible and allowing easy computation of the Jacobian determinant.

Training a normalizing flow

Let's say the transformation $F$ depends on some parameters $\varphi$.

Poisson likelihood of a set of observed points $\vec{y}$: $$ \begin{align} \ln \mathcal{L}(\varphi) &= \sum_{i=1}^N \ln p_y(\vec{y}_i \mid \varphi) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^N \left\{ \ln p_x(F_\varphi^{-1}(\vec{y}_i)) + \ln \left| \det \left( \frac{\partial F_\varphi^{-1}(\vec{y}_i)}{\partial \vec{y}_i} \right) \right| \, \right\} \, . \end{align} $$ Maximize this likelihood w.r.t. $\varphi$ to find the best-fitting transformation.

Normal machine-learning workflow: stochastic gradient descent on batches of observed points $\vec{y}$.

Discrete normalizing flows

Idea: Chain together many simple invertible transformations: $$ F = f_n \circ f_{n-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_2 \circ f_1 \, . $$

With enough transformations, we can hopefully approximate any arbitrarily complicated distribution.

The inverse is easy to construct: $$ F^{-1} = f_1^{-1} \circ f_2^{-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_{n-1}^{-1} \circ f_n^{-1} \, . $$

The Jacobian determinant is the product of the individual Jacobian determinants: $$ \left| \det \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial \vec{x}} \right) \right| = \prod_{i=1}^n \left| \det \left( \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \vec{x}_{i-1}} \right) \right| \, . $$

There are many classes of simple transformations one could choose.

Real NVP

“Affine coupling layer”

Split the input vector $\vec{x}$ into two parts: $$ \begin{align} \vec{x} = \begin{pmatrix} \vec{x}_{1: d} \\ \vec{x}_{d+1: D} \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$

Then, shift and scale only the second part, based on the first part: $$ \begin{align} \vec{y} = \begin{pmatrix} \vec{x}_{1: d} \\ \vec{x}_{d+1: D} \odot \exp \left[s\left(\vec{x}_{1: d}\right)\right]+t\left(\vec{x}_{1: d}\right) \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$

The functions $s$ and $t$, the scale and translation functions, determine the transformation.

Invertibility:

The shift and scale can be undone, because $\vec{x}_{1: d}$ is preserved and can be used to compute the inverse transformation.

Jacobian:

The Jacobian of this transformation is lower-triangular: $$ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x^T} = \left[\begin{array}{cc} \mathbb{1}_d & 0 \\ \frac{\partial y_{d+1: D}}{\partial x_{1: d}^T} & \operatorname{diag}\left(\exp \left[s\left(x_{1: d}\right)\right]\right) \end{array}\right] \, . $$

⇒ Its determinant is simply the product of the diagonal elements: $$ \operatorname{det}\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x^T}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^{D-d} \exp \left[s\left(x_{1: d}\right)_i\right] \, . $$

Dinh et al. (2016), "Density estimation using Real NVP", arXiv:1605.08803.

Continuous normalizing flows

What if we chain together ever more transformations, and make each transformation “smaller”?

Infinitesimal transformation: $\vec{z}_{i+1} = \vec{z}_i + \vec{v}(\vec{z}_i, t_i) \Delta t$.

Continuous limit: $ \begin{align} \frac{d\vec{z}}{dt} = \vec{v}(\vec{z}, t) \, . \end{align} $

Start with $\vec{z}(0) = \vec{x}$, and integrate the ODE to obtain $\vec{y} \equiv \vec{z}(1)$.

Invertibility: integrate the ODE backwards in time.

Jacobian: $$ \begin{align} \ln p_y(\vec{y}) = \ln p_x(\vec{x}) - \int_0^1 \operatorname{Tr} \left( \frac{\partial \vec{v}}{\partial \vec{z}(t')} \right) dt' \, . \end{align} $$

Chen et al. (2018), "Neural Ordinary Differential Equations", arXiv:1806.07366.

Grathwohl et al. (2018), "FFJORD: Free-form Continuous Dynamics for Scalable Reversible Generative Models", arXiv:1810.01367..

Training continuous normalizing flows

Method 1: Straightforward approach

Maximize the Poisson likelihood, as with discrete normalizing flows. Requires integrating the ODE for every sample.

Method 2: “Flow Matching”

It turns out one can learn the vector field $\vec{v}$ directly by pairing random samples from the observed data $\vec{y}$ and the base distribution $\vec{x}$, and encouraging straight paths between them. No ODE integration required!

Lipman et al. (2022), "Flow Matching for Generative Modeling", arXiv:2210.02747.

Conditional normalizing flows

Let's say we have a cosmological simulation that produces mock data $D$ for a given set of model parameters $\theta$ (such as the ΛCDM parameters).

We want to know the likelihood $p(D \mid \theta)$ so that we can infer $\theta$ from real-world data.

Idea: Run many cosmological simulations with different values of $\theta$, and then learn $p(D \mid \theta)$ as a conditional normalizing flow.

Now, the transformation inside the normalizing flow will depend on $\theta$.

This is not difficult to implement!
